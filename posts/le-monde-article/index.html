In Kenya, smallholder farmers are experimenting with AI to improve their yields

Published on November 16, 2025
Share:

Disclaimer: This is a translated English version of the Le Monde Article originally in French. Find the actual article here

Several applications using artificial intelligence, including chatbots, are popular with Kenyan farmers.

Samuel Kipyegon has long plastic boots, a blue-roofed house, six children, and 200 coffee trees planted on a hillside. His harvest “has never been as good as this year”, he exclaims in mid‑October. The 54‑year‑old farmer stands on his land in the village of Emiitiot in western Kenya.

With his calloused fingers, the man gently touches the red coffee beans of one of his trees: “Normally, my plants yield 3,000 kilos per year. This year, I’ll be at over 7,000 kilos. It’s thanks to Virtual Agronomist. It told me to use more phosphorus and to change fertilizers.”

The farmer followed the recommendations of the artificial intelligence designed to improve agricultural yields. He climbed onto the back of a motorcycle taxi and travelled five kilometers from his home to buy six 50‑kilogram bags of a fertilizer richer in phosphorus, which he then distributed around the base of his coffee trees. “The technology told me to put 100 grams per tree. Before, I would put on a few handfuls without paying attention to the quantity.”

Barnabeus Koech, 47, is also satisfied. His farm is located a few dozen kilometers from Mr. Kipyegon’s. He claims that Virtual Agronomist helped increase his corn production from 15 bags to 25 bags of 90 kilos each. “The application advised me to space my plants farther apart and use less fertilizer. It worked well.”

“Tailored advice”

In recent years, like Virtual Agronomist, several AI‑powered tools have been developed in East Africa by non-governmental organisations and startups. Digital Green and PlantVillage also provide advice to boost crop yields. The Ujuzi Kilimo platform provides agricultural data to smallholder farmers. Tulaa, Agin, and Apollo Agriculture enable smallholder farmers to access agricultural inputs, credit, and markets. Project Farm analyzes trends in agricultural activities.

Virtual Agronomist, which was developed by the NGO Innovative Solutions for Decision Agriculture (iSDA), funded in particular by the Gates Foundation (partner and financial contributor of “Le Monde Afrique”), now claims 210,000 users in Kenya. The country has 7 million smallholder farmers out of a population of 53 million. In 2004, the agricultural sector still represented 20% of the gross domestic product. Virtual Agronomist, also present in Uganda, Tanzania, and Malawi, and being tested in Nigeria and Rwanda, is not an application but a chatbot using AI — essentially a simple WhatsApp conversation with an AI designed by agronomists.

“Virtual Agronomist provides tailored advice for each farm. It’s very precise, whereas other tools only offer score simulations at the village or county level,” explains one of the project’s members. Other tools used in Kenya rely on photos taken by farmers.

African Champion of Technologies

Emmanuel Kiplagat Korir, a tall young man in his twenties, wearing a black jacket, pulls a phone from his pocket. He is one of Virtual Agronomist’s “farmer trainers”. He is responsible for explaining to smallholder farmers, often unfamiliar with new digital technologies, how to use the tool. He opens WhatsApp and answers a series of questions. What crop is being grown this season? Where is the village located? What is the soil type? What is the spacing between the plants?

The AI asks the farmer to take a little soil in his hands and describe it: dry, sticky, light, dark, crumbly, compact. This data, combined with the GPS location of the plot and the history of surrounding plantings, makes it possible to establish a tailor‑made diagnosis of the needs to improve production.

A report by the Global System for Mobile Communications, published in July 2024, shows that most AI applications in Kenya are related to agriculture and food security. The document highlights the potential of the agricultural sector but also emphasizes the need to democratize access to smartphones and provide adequate training for their users.

In July 2025, another report from the data analytics firm DataReportal and the agency Meltwater showed that Kenyans were the world’s leading users of ChatGPT…ahead of countries such as the United States, China, and Japan. According to the study, more than 42% of Kenyans had used ChatGPT in the previous month.

Strengthening Hierarchies

Since the 2000s, Kenya has established itself as an African champion of new technologies, thanks to the success of Safaricom, its telecom operator, which popularized mobile payments. The country is sometimes nicknamed “Silicon Savannah,” a nod to Silicon Valley. In 2023, President William Ruto even announced that all primary school students in the country would now learn computer coding.

In the field of agriculture, however, some academics question the use of AI. In an article for the journal Smart Agricultural Technology, published in March 2023, five researchers, including Kenyan Angeline Wairegi, question the unbridled “technological optimism.” According to them, smart agricultural technologies (…) can constitute a new system (…) of appropriation and control of the labor and knowledge of small‑scale women farmers in East Africa.” AI risks “reinforcing hierarchies and ignoring indigenous knowledge and ways of life.”

In the village of Emiitiot, amidst his coffee trees, Mr. Kipyegon raises another concern: the poor internet connection in his village. While Kenya has 5G, disparities between urban and rural areas remain significant. He is, however, optimistic: with his production expected to increase in 2025, he plans to buy new coffee trees.
